Triva- Thomson Reuters

Project Context

Triva, or the Thomson Reuters Intelligent Virtual Agent, is a chatbot used for three customer segments: small business accountants, corporate accountants and legal professionals. They were created in June 2020 during COVID. I came in September 2022 and I joined when the company was working on adding new use cases. I worked with a Scrum team that included 12 development team members, 3 ops members (originally), 1 product manager, 1 product/UI designer and 1 other conversation designer.

My daily tasks included: creating and updating different use cases and states (error states, live agent hours, etc). I also worked on side projects such as: a competitive analysis, NLU research (failure), incorporating Acrolinx (a grammar AI tool), etc to find ways to improve the chatbot.

Problem

Triva was a growing chatbot that needed new use cases or had use cases that were updated. Also, there were always areas of improvements needed for that chatbot.

Solution

Find ways to create use cases and improve the chatbot

Daily Tasks

Customers felt frustrated waiting long queues. This led to the creation of the first chatbot. As a conversation designer, my task was to create new uses for the chatbot. This is the process

My team and I looked for areas of improvement and brought ideas to meetings. After discussion about the changes needed, my counterpart or I would update the design on LucidSpark and get approval from our project manager.

I also created two repositories for documentation. The first repository was for best practice articles. Best practice articles focused on the following topics: connection between UX and conversation design, NLU best practices, user personas for different bots, Triva’s bot persona (my manager wrote this for a presentation earlier), etc.

The second repository was the bot’s flow documentation. For flow documentation, my colleague and I created a team repository on LucidSpark. We also documented every path a user can take on each bot. The documentation was updated when changes were made to the bot.

Limitations

Working in a large corporation brought new learnings: limited interaction due to silos, indirect customer understanding through SMEs and support agents, and a shift from immediate user feedback to observing impact through leadership and dashboards. I also had to independently maintain UX skills with tools like Figma. My failures included focusing on one-off terms in NLU updates, using incorrect customer terms in chatbots, delivering poor developer feedback, and ultimately experiencing a layoff.

Conclusion

My time at Thomson Reuters as a conversation designer was incredible. It reignited my passion for the field, allowing me to hone my skills and build use cases for diverse customer segments across legal and accounting. As a team member, I learned the ropes of corporate environments and witnessed the growing importance of AI in conversation design.
